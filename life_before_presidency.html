Life Before Presidency

Early Career and Military Engagements

Washington began his career as a land surveyor at the age of 16, gaining knowledge of the Virginia frontier, which would later aid his military leadership. He served as a lieutenant colonel in the Virginia militia during the French and Indian War (1754â€“1763), where his experiences shaped his military tactics. His first military engagement at Fort Necessity earned him recognition, and he later served as an aide-de-camp to General Braddock during the Battle of Monongahela, helping rally British forces after Braddock's death.

Commander-in-Chief and Revolutionary War

After the battles of Lexington and Concord, Washington was appointed commander-in-chief of the Continental Army by the Second Continental Congress in 1775. He took command during the Siege of Boston, forcing the British to evacuate in March 1776. His bold decision to cross the Delaware River on Christmas night and attack the Hessian forces in Trenton resulted in a significant victory, boosting morale for the Continental Army. Washington's leadership during the harsh winter at Valley Forge helped transform the army into a more disciplined force.

Alliances and Victories

Washington played a crucial role in strengthening ties with France, which provided military and financial support to the colonial cause. His leadership was pivotal in the victory at Saratoga, convincing France to formally ally with the United States. Washington's strategic management during the Southern Campaign and the Siege of Yorktown ultimately led to British defeat and the end of the Revolutionary War. After the war, he famously resigned his commission, setting a precedent for civilian control of the military.
