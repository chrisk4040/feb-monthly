Life During Presidency

Accomplishments

George Washington's presidency (1789â€“1797) was marked by significant accomplishments that laid the foundation for the United States. He established the new government by creating executive departments, including the Department of State, Treasury, and War, and signed the Judiciary Act of 1789, which formed the federal judiciary system. Washington also supported the formation of the Bill of Rights, ensuring the protection of individual liberties and limiting government power.

Financial Foundations

During his presidency, Washington focused on building financial and economic foundations. He supported Alexander Hamilton's financial plan, which included the creation of a national bank and the assumption of state debts. Washington signed the Mint Act, establishing the U.S. Mint, and endorsed tariffs and excise taxes to fund the federal government. His foreign policy was characterized by neutrality, as he issued the Proclamation of Neutrality in 1793 and negotiated treaties with Britain and Spain to stabilize relations and secure trade routes.

Leadership Precedents

Washington's leadership set important precedents for the nation. He established the two-term limit for presidents and defined the title "Mr. President," emphasizing the republican ideals of the new government. His Farewell Address in 1796 advised against political parties and foreign entanglements, guiding future American policies. Washington's voluntary decision to step down after two terms demonstrated his commitment to democracy and set a standard for future leaders.
